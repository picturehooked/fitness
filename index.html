Loading Principles -- Both Phases

2 Reps in Reserve

Every set should finish with 2 reps still available. Failure means the weight is too heavy. 4+ easy means increase it.

Form Before Load

Particularly on hip hinges. A rounded lower back under load is exactly the injury you are trying to prevent.

Progressive Overload

In Phase 2, add the smallest available increment when you hit the top of the rep range cleanly across all sets for two sessions running.

Rest Periods

Phase 1: 60-90 seconds between sets. Phase 2: 45-60 seconds. Shorter rest is part of the progression.

Session 1 (Mobility)

Bodyweight and bands only. No calibration needed. The measure is range of motion and control, not load.

Logging

Write weight and reps for every loaded set. You cannot progress without a record. A notes app is enough.

Session 1 -- Tuesday PM (post-ride)

MOBILITY & ACTIVATION

30 minBodyweight + bands

Targets the three cycling failure modes: stiff thoracic spine, locked hip flexors, and inhibited glutes. Done immediately post-ride while tissue is warm. Replaces post-ride stretching. Movement quality is the only measure -- no load required.

Same exercises both phases -- progression is range of motion, not load
ExerciseEquipmentSets / Reps
Thoracic Spine
Thoracic rotation
Kneeling or seated, hands behind head. Rotate upper back only -- hips stay square. Pause at end range.
Cue: imagine your upper back is a wrung towel. The lumbar spine does not move.
Bodyweight2 x 10 each side
Cat-cow with thoracic extension
On all fours. Cycle between rounding and extending. On the extension phase, pause 2 seconds and breathe into the upper back.
Cue: in extension, think about opening your chest to the ceiling. Slow is more effective than fast.
Bodyweight2 x 8 slow
Hip Flexors
90/90 hip stretch
Both legs at 90 degrees on the floor. Hinge forward over the front shin. Hold -- do not pulse. The deepest hip flexors only release under sustained hold.
Cue: sit tall first, then hinge. If your pelvis tips back as you lean, sit on a folded towel to raise the hips.
Bodyweight2 x 45 sec each side
Kneeling hip flexor with overhead reach
Back knee down on the floor, front foot flat. Drive the hip forward. Reach the same-side arm overhead and lean slightly away.
Cue: squeeze the glute of the kneeling leg throughout. If you lose that squeeze, you lose the stretch.
Bodyweight2 x 40 sec each side
Glute Activation
Glute bridge with band
Band above knees. Lie on back, feet flat. Push into the band as you lift -- the lateral tension is the point. Hold 2 seconds at the top.
Cue: do not let the knees cave inward. If they do, the band is too strong. Drive knees out against it throughout.
Band3 x 12
Clamshell
Band above knees. Side-lying, knees bent at 90 degrees. Rotate the top knee toward the ceiling. Pelvis stays completely still.
Cue: place your top hand on your hip. If you feel the pelvis rolling back, you are using the wrong muscles -- reduce the range.
Band2 x 15 each side
Flow and Finish
World's greatest stretch
Lunge position, same-side elbow to the floor, then rotate that arm to the ceiling. One movement, many targets. Do it slowly.
Cue: the elbow-to-floor position is the anchor. From there, the rotation should feel like opening a door with your chest, not just reaching your arm up.
Bodyweight2 x 5 each side
Child's pose to downward dog
Flow slowly between the two positions. Breathe into areas of restriction. This is the deliberate wind-down -- do not rush it.
Cue: in downward dog, press the floor away and try to lengthen the spine rather than just pushing the hips up.
Bodyweight2 x 6 transitions

Notes

  • This session does not change between phases. The progression is increased range of motion and reduced compensation -- not load.
  • Done within 30 minutes of finishing a ride, the hip flexor work in particular will feel significantly more effective than cold stretching.
Session 2 -- Thursday

UPPER BODY & CORE

35 minDumbbells + bands

Loads the pulling and rotational patterns cycling never touches. Pull volume deliberately exceeds push at a 2:1 ratio -- the correct response to cycling's anteriorly dominant position. Trains the core to resist load rather than flex under it.

Phase 1 -- Weeks 1 to 4 -- Foundation
ExerciseEquipmentSets / Reps
Warm-Up
Band pull-apart
Arms straight at chest height. Pull band apart until it reaches your sternum. Squeeze shoulder blades at the back.
Cue: keep the arms straight throughout. Bent elbows turn this into a bicep exercise, not a shoulder blade one.
Band2 x 15
Arm circles and shoulder rolls
Large slow circles forward and backward. Follow with shoulder rolls. Unloaded preparation of the shoulder joint.
Cue: make the circles as large as comfortable. Small circles achieve almost nothing here.
Bodyweight1 x 60 sec
Pull -- Priority Movement
Dumbbell single-arm row
Brace on bench or knee with opposite hand. Pull elbow to hip -- not to shoulder. The elbow drives the movement, not the hand.
Cue: at the top of the row, hold for 1 second and feel the shoulder blade move toward the spine. That contraction is the point of the exercise.
Dumbbell3 x 8 each arm
Band face pull
Band at face height. Pull to forehead with elbows high and wide. Externally rotate the shoulder at the end position.
Cue: elbows must be at or above shoulder height throughout. Dropping the elbows turns this into a row, not a face pull.
Band3 x 12
Push -- Counterbalance
Dumbbell floor press
Lie on floor, elbows at 45 degrees from the body. Press up until arms are extended. Lower until upper arms touch the floor.
Cue: do not let the elbows flare to 90 degrees -- that loads the shoulder joint, not the chest. 45 degrees is the correct angle.
Dumbbells3 x 10
Seated dumbbell overhead press
Seated upright. Start with dumbbells at shoulder height, press to lockout overhead. Control the descent fully.
Cue: brace your core before every rep. Do not arch the lower back to complete the press -- if you need to arch, the weight is too heavy.
Dumbbells2 x 10
Core -- Anti-Rotation
Dead bug
Lie on back. Arms pointing to ceiling, knees at 90 degrees. Slowly extend opposite arm and leg toward the floor. Lower back must stay pressed down throughout.
Cue: if your lower back lifts off the floor at any point, the movement is too large. Reduce the range until you can maintain contact.
Bodyweight3 x 8 each side
Forearm plank
Forearms on floor, body in a straight line from head to heel. Squeeze glutes and brace core simultaneously.
Cue: squeezing the glutes is not optional -- it is half the exercise. A plank without glute engagement is mostly lower back endurance, which is not what we are training here.
Bodyweight3 x 30 sec
Phase 2 -- Weeks 5 to 8 -- Progressive Overload
ExerciseEquipmentSets / Reps
Warm-Up
Band pull-apart
Heavier band or double up. 3 sets now rather than 2.
Cue: same as Phase 1 -- straight arms, shoulder blades moving.
Band (heavier)3 x 15
Pull -- Increased Load
Dumbbell single-arm row
Heavier than Phase 1. 6-rep target means genuine effort on every set. Rest only 45 seconds between sets.
Cue: same 1-second hold at the top. Heavier weight, shorter rest.
Dumbbell (heavier)4 x 6 each arm
Band face pull
Heavier band tension. Maintain form over load -- the shoulder external rotation is the priority.
Cue: elbows high, hold the end position for 1 second.
Band (heavier)3 x 15
Renegade row
Plank on two dumbbells. Row one at a time. The challenge is keeping the hips square -- that is the core work. Phase 2 addition only.
Cue: before each row, brace hard and imagine someone is about to push your hip sideways. Resist that imaginary force as you pull.
Dumbbells3 x 6 each arm
Push -- Progressed
Dumbbell floor press
Heavier than Phase 1. Same form, more load.
Cue: 45-degree elbow angle. No flaring.
Dumbbells (heavier)3 x 8
Standing dumbbell overhead press
Standing now -- adds a significant core stability demand. Brace fully before initiating every single rep.
Cue: stand with feet hip-width. Before pressing, take a breath into your belly and brace as if you are about to take a punch. Press from that position.
Dumbbells3 x 8
Core -- Progressed
Dead bug with dumbbell
Hold a light dumbbell in the extending arm. Significantly increases the anti-extension demand. Start lighter than you expect to need.
Cue: same lower back rule as Phase 1. The dumbbell adds demand -- if your back lifts, drop the weight.
Light dumbbell3 x 8 each side
Side plank
Forearm on floor, body in a straight line sideways. Hips stay lifted. Replaces standard plank -- lateral core loading that cycling does nothing to develop.
Cue: most people let the hips sag or rotate forward. Stack them directly above each other and keep the top hip driving toward the ceiling.
Bodyweight3 x 35 sec each side

Notes

  • Pull volume exceeds push at roughly 2:1. This is intentional -- it directly addresses cycling's anterior dominance and rounded-shoulder position.
  • Rest 60-90 seconds in Phase 1, 45-60 in Phase 2. The shorter rest is part of the overload stimulus -- not an efficiency measure.
Session 3 -- Sunday

LOWER BODY & POSTERIOR CHAIN

35 minKettlebells + barbell

Hip hinge, single-leg strength, and glute loading. Corrects the quad-dominant cycling pattern, removes compressive load from the lower back, and transfers directly to power on the bike. Placed on Sunday for 24 hours of recovery from the Saturday ride.

Phase 1 -- Weeks 1 to 4 -- Foundation
ExerciseEquipmentSets / Reps
Warm-Up
Glute bridge (bodyweight)
Lie on back, feet flat. Push hips to ceiling, squeeze glutes at top, hold 2 seconds. Pre-activates glutes before loading them.
Cue: do not skip this. Cold glutes under a loaded deadlift means the lower back compensates. 2 minutes here prevents the problem.
Bodyweight2 x 12
Standing hip circles
Stand on one foot. Draw large slow circles with the raised knee. Forward and backward. Mobilises the hip joint before loading.
Cue: make the circles as large as balance allows. The range is the point.
Bodyweight1 x 10 each side
Hip Hinge -- Priority Pattern
Kettlebell deadlift
One or two kettlebells between feet. Push the floor away rather than pulling the weight up. Glutes drive the lockout at the top -- do not hyperextend the back.
Cue: at setup, think "proud chest, hips back." If the lower back rounds at any point, the weight is too heavy or the hinge pattern needs more work.
Kettlebells4 x 6
Romanian deadlift (barbell)
Bar only (17.5kg) in Phase 1. Hinge from the hips with soft knees. Push the hips back until you feel the hamstrings load. Return by driving the hips forward.
Cue: let the bar stay close to the legs throughout -- it should almost brush the shins and thighs on the way down. If it swings away from you, you have lost the hinge.
Barbell (17.5kg)3 x 8
Single-Leg -- Cycling Transfer
Reverse lunge
Step back -- not forward. Back knee descends toward the floor, front knee stays over the front foot. Dumbbells at sides.
Cue: the front shin should be close to vertical. If the front knee shoots forward over the toes, your stride is too short -- step back further.
Dumbbells3 x 8 each leg
Single-leg deadlift (bodyweight)
Stand on one leg. Hinge forward as the back leg rises behind you for counterbalance. Arms hang or extend forward. Balance is the challenge, not load.
Cue: keep a soft bend in the standing knee. A locked knee makes balance harder and shortens the hamstring range. Think about your standing hip staying square to the floor.
Bodyweight2 x 6 each side
Squat Pattern
Goblet squat
Hold kettlebell at chest. Feet shoulder-width with slight outward toe angle. Sit between the knees -- elbows push them out at the bottom.
Cue: at the bottom, your chest should stay tall and your weight should be through the whole foot. If your heels rise, you need to work on ankle mobility or widen the stance slightly.
Kettlebell3 x 10
Phase 2 -- Weeks 5 to 8 -- Progressive Overload
ExerciseEquipmentSets / Reps
Warm-Up
Banded glute bridge
Band above knees replaces bodyweight version. Lateral tension increases glute medius activation.
Cue: drive knees outward against the band as you press the hips up. Both movements simultaneously.
Band2 x 15
Hip Hinge -- Loaded
Kettlebell deadlift
Heavier than Phase 1. 5-rep target at near-maximum controllable load. Form is the ceiling -- reduce weight immediately if the hinge breaks.
Cue: same proud chest cue as Phase 1. Heavier weight, 4 sets, shorter rest (45 sec).
Kettlebells (heavier)4 x 5
Romanian deadlift (barbell)
Add weight to the bar now that hinge mechanics are established. Increase by small increments only -- this movement earns load slowly.
Cue: bar stays close to the legs. Hamstrings should be loaded by the time the bar reaches just below knee height.
Barbell (added load)4 x 6
Kettlebell swing
Hip hinge under power -- not a squat. The bell is driven by the hips, not pulled by the arms. At the top, stand tall with glutes squeezed hard. Phase 2 addition only.
Cue: think of the swing as a horizontal jump -- all the power comes from snapping the hips forward. The arms are just a connection to the bell, nothing more.
Kettlebell3 x 12
Single-Leg -- Loaded
Reverse lunge
Heavier than Phase 1. Same controlled tempo -- additional load should not change the movement quality.
Cue: if the front knee wobbles inward under load, lighten the weight. Knee tracking is non-negotiable.
Dumbbells (heavier)3 x 8 each leg
Single-leg RDL with kettlebell
Hold a kettlebell in the opposite hand to the working leg. Balance from Phase 1 means load can now be added. The highest-value single-leg exercise in this programme.
Cue: the kettlebell hand hangs directly below the shoulder. Think about keeping your hips square to the floor as you hinge -- the tendency is to open the hip of the raised leg.
Kettlebell3 x 6 each side
Squat Pattern -- Progressed
Heavy goblet squat
Heavier kettlebell. Same movement. The front squat keeps the torso upright -- better for cyclists than a back squat position.
Cue: at the bottom, pause for 1 second. This eliminates the bounce reflex and forces genuine muscular effort to stand up.
Kettlebell (heavier)4 x 8

Notes

  • The barbell RDL starts at bar only regardless of how strong you feel. Two consecutive clean sessions before adding any load.
  • The kettlebell swing in Phase 2 is the highest-transfer exercise in this programme for cycling power. It earns a permanent place beyond Week 8.
  • Single-leg work outranks bilateral squats for cycling transfer. The pedal stroke is a unilateral movement. Do not sacrifice it for heavier bilateral lifts.
